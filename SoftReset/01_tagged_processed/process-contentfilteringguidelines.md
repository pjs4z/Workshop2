## Content Filtering Guidelines

The rubric defines a five-level scale for rating content that progresses from generic filler to rare, ethos-defining brilliance. Level one denotes predictable material lacking insight; level two shows slight distinctiveness without depth; level three delivers solid narrative coherence and clear alignment with at least one core value; level four demonstrates marked originality, emotional or intellectual richness, and strong value alignment; level five is reserved for exceptional passages whose resonance can anchor the organisation’s identity.

To preserve grading integrity, the framework dictates an approximate distribution: levels five and one should each appear in only a small fraction of cases, level four in roughly a quarter, level three in about half, and level two in the remaining slice. This statistical guardrail curbs score inflation and signals that most content will cluster around competent distinctiveness, while true excellence remains uncommon.

Assessors are urged to exercise rigorous judgment, constantly testing whether a passage is merely competent or genuinely outstanding. Referencing established high- and low-water marks, they must apply scores consistently and resist defaulting to higher ratings, ensuring that the scale retains meaning and that exceptional work is recognised without dilution.
