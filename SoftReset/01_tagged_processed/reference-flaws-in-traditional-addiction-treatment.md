## Flaws in Traditional Addiction Treatment

For more than half a century, American addiction care has revolved around the twelve-step template, a faith-tinged, abstinence-only regimen whose outcomes remain dismal. Large-scale analyses place its long-term success in the single digits, yet the program’s literature locates failure in personal defects rather than in its own doctrine. By insisting that recovery begins with surrender to a Higher Power and a permanent confession of powerlessness, it binds participants to a fixed “alcoholic” identity that corrodes agency and hope.

The dogma tolerates no nuance: lifelong abstinence is the sole metric of virtue, and any slip is framed as total relapse. This absolutism converts minor lapses into crises, shames those who struggle, and excludes harm-reduction or moderation strategies that might keep people alive while they build stability.

Despite advances in neuroscience and psychology, twelve-step ideology still dominates roughly ninety percent of U.S. rehabs. Many facilities offer little beyond group confession and a few vintage steps because that model is inexpensive, easily reimbursed, and legally familiar. Counselors are often former patients with scant clinical training; research that deviates from the orthodoxy has historically been starved of funding and airtime.

Structural forces reinforce the stagnation. Providers, insurers, and pharmaceutical firms fear the liability of innovation in a litigious environment, so they retreat to familiar but ineffective routines. Commercial incentives for new medications are weak, reputational risks are high, and insurance protocols reward standardized care over tailored treatment. As the drug supply grows more lethal, fewer than one in ten people with a substance-use disorder receive any help at all, and most who do are funneled into the same brittle script.

The prevailing system treats addiction as an incurable spiritual affliction rather than a complex, treatable condition. By relying on moral indoctrination instead of individualized, evidence-based care, it perpetuates harm and blocks progress. Reset arose from the recognition that this paradigm is scientifically obsolete and ethically untenable—and that a rigorous, patient-centered alternative is urgently required.
