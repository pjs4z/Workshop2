## Memantine for ADHD and Stimulant Misuse

Memantine, an NMDA-receptor antagonist long used in neurodegenerative disease, is attracting interest as a means to stabilize the glutamate–dopamine network that underlies both core ADHD symptoms and the cascade of neural adaptations that drive stimulant tolerance and misuse. By tempering excessive glutamatergic signaling it indirectly normalizes dopamine transmission, flattening the peaks and troughs that feed impulsivity, craving, and dose escalation while also shielding neurons from excitotoxic stress. This mechanism positions the drug as either a stand-alone alternative for individuals who cannot tolerate standard stimulants or, more commonly, as an adjunct intended to prolong the therapeutic window of existing stimulant regimens and reduce their reinforcing “rush.”

Preliminary clinical work and convergent real-world experience show that memantine can produce modest but meaningful gains in attention, planning, and behavioral inhibition, and that it often slows or even halts the rise of stimulant tolerance without adding cardiovascular risk or abuse liability. Most patients experience only transient dizziness or appetite loss, yet a minority report cognitive dulling or paradoxical disinhibition, underscoring the need for careful titration. Evidence is weaker for direct craving reduction in entrenched addiction, suggesting the drug’s value lies more in prevention and harm mitigation than in acute detoxification. Overall, memantine offers a low-profile, mechanistically distinct lever for clinicians seeking longer-term stability in ADHD management and a protective buffer against the spiral of stimulant overuse, pending confirmation from larger controlled trials.
