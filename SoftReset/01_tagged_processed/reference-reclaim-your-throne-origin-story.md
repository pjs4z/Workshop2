## Reclaim Your Throne Origin Story

At nineteen I strode through Yale’s arches certain the world was already mine. Professors applauded, my parents boasted, and every accolade fed a private conviction that destiny waited on my signal. To keep pace with the praise I turned to Adderall—first a clever hack, then an invisible leash—masking fatigue while eroding the self I believed unshakable. Graduation delivered no crown, only a harsher arena. The pills kept me wired, whiskey numbed the crash, and the résumé that once gleamed became a disguise for nights spent trembling beside empty bottles. Friends advanced, parents worried, and I hid behind half-truths until even my own reflection felt like a stranger. Only Yoda, my squat French bulldog, met me without judgment, his morning nudges a daily reminder that some part of life still expected me to show up.

The fall bottomed out on a rain-slicked alley floor where I dialed my father, hung up, and finally wished the night would swallow me. Instead, a massive bear in a torn leather jacket emerged from the shadows, sat across from me, and spoke with the calm authority of a lifelong coach. He named the pills and booze for what they were—false promises of dopamine—and urged a gentler interrogation: not “What’s wrong with you?” but “Why the pain?” In a conjured vision he revealed my inner kingdom, charred and vacant yet crowned by a solitary golden throne entwined in thorns. The message was stark: the seat remained mine, but reclamation required a fight, not an apology. Around the pillars I glimpsed allies—my parents, long-lost friends, loyal Yoda—waiting to cheer the first honest step.

That night I accepted the bear’s paw, stood, and felt the faint pulse of possibility. By morning the alley was memory, but a damp, folded note lay in my hand: Reclaim your throne. The absence of a hangover felt miraculous; the presence of hope felt even stranger. I called my father, confessed the truth, and asked for help with the steady voice of someone finally ready to earn his own rescue. The note now hangs on my refrigerator, a daily decree that the battle ahead is mine alone to wage but never again alone to face. I am not yet seated; I am merely upright. Yet every sober breath, every walk with Yoda, every act of repair is a step up those cracked stone stairs toward the life I once imagined and refuse to forfeit.
