## Our Views on Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) and Addiction Treatment Paradigms

Addiction treatment in the United States still leans on a framework conceived in the mid-1930s, when Alcoholics Anonymous cast compulsive drinking as a spiritual malady to be relieved by surrender to a Higher Power. That model was groundbreaking for its era and later underpinned the insurance-friendly “disease” narrative adopted by rehab centers, but its core assumptions—lifelong powerlessness, confession of moral defects, reliance on faith—have remained largely unchanged despite decades of progress in neuroscience, psychology, and pharmacology. The result is a dominant, quasi-religious protocol with modest long-term success rates that continues to eclipse more contemporary, evidence-based options.

The Reset Order positions itself as the corrective to this stagnation. Grounded in modern brain science, behavioral theory, trauma-informed care, and data-driven coaching, it treats addiction as a neurobehavioral loop that can be rewired rather than a fixed spiritual affliction. Instead of urging surrender, it cultivates personal agency; instead of invoking divine intervention, it deploys measurable interventions that strengthen executive function and reshape reward circuitry. By replacing mid-century dogma with demonstrable methods and technology-enabled support, the program aims to give individuals the power to conquer dependency and move forward without the lifelong identity of “recovering addict.”
