## The Reset Order Doctrine: Addiction as a Coping Mechanism

The doctrine positions stimulant and behavioral addiction as a secondary adaptation to unresolved emotional pain, asserting that people reach for dopamine-driven highs to mute chronic feelings of inadequacy, fear, loneliness, or grief that often trace back to early adversity or relentless performance pressure. By asking why the pain rather than why the drug, it recasts compulsive use as a logical but costly attempt at self-protection, thereby replacing judgment with informed empathy.

Accordingly, the program concentrates on exposing and healing those wounds through guided reflection, relational support, and deliberate cultivation of healthy pleasure and purpose. Cravings and relapses are mined for insight into unmet needs, self-compassion displaces shame, and participants practice new coping skills until authentic security, validation, achievement, intimacy, and expression render the stimulant obsolete. In dissolving the original hurt, the approach removes the addiction’s utility, allowing members to abandon the substance because they no longer need it, not merely because they are told to stop.
