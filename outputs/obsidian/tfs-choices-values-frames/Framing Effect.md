# Framing Effect

**Definition:** The cognitive phenomenon where objectively equivalent versions of a choice problem elicit drastically different preferences depending on how the outcomes and contingencies are described.

## Systems Dynamics & Relationships
The [[Framing Effect]] occurs when the semantic presentation of a problem alters the baseline reference point used by the [[Value Function]]. It actively disrupts rational choice models by causing preferences to shift based on wording rather than mathematical expectation.
- **Upstream (Driven by):** Semantic formulation, Environmental presentation (e.g., 'lives saved' vs. 'lives lost')
- **Downstream (Drives):** Inhibits the [[Invariance Principle]], triggers the [[Pseudo-certainty Effect]]

## Behavioral Engineering Context (COM-B)
- **Opportunity:** The structural presentation of choices provides the opportunity to externally shape decisions. Choice architects can manipulate whether an option feels like a gain or a loss (e.g., labeling a price difference as a 'cash discount' vs. a 'credit surcharge').
- **Capability:** Highlights a core cognitive limitation—humans lack the intuitive capability to spontaneously translate objectively equivalent messages into a canonical, abstract representation.
- **Leverage Points:** Systematically present critical decision problems in multiple, contrasting frames simultaneously (e.g., displaying both mortality and survival rates) to neutralize the automatic [[Framing Effect]] and force reflective, invariant choices.
