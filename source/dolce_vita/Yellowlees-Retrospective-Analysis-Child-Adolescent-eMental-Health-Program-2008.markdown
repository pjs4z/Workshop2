## A Retrospective Analysis of a Child and Adolescent eMental Health Program

PETER M. YELLOWLEES, M.D., M.B.B.S., DONALD M. HILTY, M.D., SHAYNA L. MARKS, B.A., JONATHAN NEUFELD, PH.D., AND JAMES A. BOURGEOIS, M.D., O.D.

## ABSTRACT

Objective: New models of psychiatric intervention are needed to improve the accessibility of mental health care in the primary care setting, particularly in rural areas of the United States and especially for children and adolescents. The aim of this study was to examine the diagnostic characteristics and outcomes for children referred for eMental Health consultations at UC Davis (videoconferencing, telephone, and secure e-mail) from 10 primary care clinics in rural northern California. Method: A retrospective analysis was conducted on the diagnostic and clinical outcomes of 139 referred children who received a full psychiatric diagnostic evaluation via videoconferencing. Within the group, a convenience sample of 58 initial and 41 three-month follow-up Child Behavior Checklists (CBCLs) was collected. Results: Comprehensive eMental Health programs appear to be effective for psychiatric diagnosis and assessment of children. Attention deficit (36.2%) and mood (28.1%) disorders were the most common diagnostic groupings overall. Most children were seen only once, but a statistically significant improvement between initial evaluation and 3-month follow-up in the convenience sample was seen in the Affect and Oppositional domains of the CBCL for girls and boys, respectively. Conclusions: Versatile eMental Health programs, incorporating standardized checklists, may assist in diagnosis and treatment of rural children. J. Am. Acad. Child Adolesc. Psychiatry , 2008;47(1):103 Y 107. Key Words: telepsychiatry, rural, eMental Health, consultation-liaison psychiatry.

Unfortunately, the research literature on telepsychiatry for children and adolescents is limited. The most comprehensive current review article on videoconferencing with children described only 27 studies, with 24 falling into the lowest quality of evidence criteria (category III). 3 Only two of the reviewed studies were randomized controlled trials of child telepsychiatry. In those two controlled experiments, clinical outcomes and diagnostic reliability were examined. Nelson and colleagues 5 described 28 depressed children ages

8 to 14. Although their small sample size is susceptible to type I errors, Nelson and colleagues 5 found that symptoms decreased significantly more in the group who received videoconference therapy than in the group who received face-to-face therapy. Elford and colleagues 6 described 23 children seen by the Memorial University of Newfoundland in a randomized controlled study examining the reliability of child and adolescent psychiatric assessments. Diagnoses and treatment recommendations were similar in 96% of the videoconference and face-to-face sessions. Other literature regarding children and telepsychiatry reveals high provider and patient satisfaction. 7 Y 13

In their 2006 analysis, Savin and colleagues 14 report the case mix of children seen in an Indian Health Service telepsychiatry consultation program in two midwestern areas. They concluded that telepsychiatry was beneficial in augmenting mental health services to children, at least in the American Indian population. However, to our knowledge, there have been few other studies that have examined the case mix and clinical profile of consecutively referred children seen over telemedicine, especially from diverse rural areas. Reviewing the case mix of any population of patients assists in not only understanding the service needs of the community but also the consultative expertise that is required to assist in its treatment and the areas for educational development of the PCPs servicing that community. Equally important, there have been no studies of clinical outcomes in the use of telepsychiatry with children, 3 despite calls for new models of service delivery, including some by technology. 15 PCPs use a number of psychiatric consultation-liaison models, some with mental health extenders and a stepped care approach to care to judiciously conserve scarce psychiatric resources. Providing these resources via telemedicine can increase access and just-in-time learning on the part of the PCPs, thereby improving their ability to care for their patients on their own.

The present study examined the characteristics and diagnoses of children referred to telepsychiatric consultation from their primary care providers in rural northern California and, in a convenience sample, also examined the change in scores on the Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL) between the time of intake and 3 months postconsultation. The study data were collected as part of a larger study, the eMental Health (eMH) Study, described elsewhere, 16 which provided multi- disciplinary consultations by videoconference, telephone, and e-mail, as well as provider education.

## METHOD

## Setting and Subjects

The UC Davis Health System is based in Sacramento, CA, and encompasses a 33-county area reaching from the geographic center of California in the central valley to the California-Oregon and California-Nevada state borders. The UC Davis Health System Center for Health and Technology provides telemedical care in 20 specialties to approximately 80 rural clinics 100 to 350 miles away. Rural is defined by the U.S. Census Bureau ( http://www.raconline. org/info\_guides/ruraldef ). Videoconferencing systems within the UC Davis Health System network work at transmission speeds of 128 to 384 kilobits per second; capital cost for the units was $8,000 to $12,000 each and the hourly cost of telephone lines ranged from $30 to $60 for the integrated services digital network. Over the course of the project, many of the older ISDN lines were replaced by less expensive, perpetually available broadband telecommunications systems.

The study population consisted of all consecutive child patients referred for a first-time telepsychiatric or telepsychological consultation at the UC Davis Medical Center from rural or suburban primary care sites from July 2004 to June 2005. Their parents or guardians gave written informed consent, and demographic information was recorded.

UCDavis Medical Center staff (two psychiatrists and one clinical psychologist) conducted diagnostic assessments over videoconferencing systems. All of the consultative services were funded by a grant from the California Telemedicine and E-health Center ( www.cteconline.org ). The services delivered by the psychiatrists followed a consultation-liaison model, with 60-minute initial consultations and 30-minute follow-up consultations. The clinical psychologist, whose services often involved psychotherapy, conducted only 60-minute sessions for all of the initial and follow-up consultations.

The complete eMH is described in detail elsewhere. 16 The present article reports on 139 consecutive pediatric teleconsultations to 10 rural health care systems (two hospitals and eight primary care clinics). Each time a PCP sought a psychiatric consultation, he or she could opt for either a curbside consultation via telephone or secure messaging or a live, two-way videoconference consultation using the traditional consultation-liaison model. 15 Y 17 Patients were not denied services because of inability to pay, and patients of all ages were accepted. For the purposes of ensuring complete data, diagnostic information was only gathered for the video-based consultations, and it is this group of patients that is described in this article. During this sample frame, CBCL data were also collected from a convenience sample of 58 consecutive children receiving an initial telepsychiatric assessment at intake and at 3 months postconsultation. Other aspects of the eMH program, including service utilization, PCP education, and adult diagnoses and outcomes have been described previously. 16

## Measures

The CBCL ( www.aseba.org ) has a wide range of clinical domains and is used to assess clinical outcomes in children ages 6 to 17. The CBCL has 118 items representing specific behavioral and emotional

problems. Parents or guardians rate their child on each item with regard to how true the statement is within the past 6 months using the following scale: 0 = not true (as far as you know); 1 = somewhat or sometimes true; 2 = very true or often true. The CBCL provides summary scores for three competence scales (Activities, Social, and School) and Total Competence. It assesses eight behavioral syndromes, including aggression, anxious/ depressed, inattention, breaking rules, social problems, somatic complaints, thought problems, withdrawn/depressed. It also provides six DSM -oriented scales/problems: affective, anxiety, somatic, attention-deficit/hyperactivity, oppositional defiant, and conduct. The scales are based on a factor analysis of parents \_ ratings of 4,994 clinically referred children, and norms are based on scores from 1,753 children ages 6 to 18. The normative sample was representative of the 48 contiguous states for socioeconomic status, ethnicity, and urban/suburban/rural residence.

## Design and Procedures

Upon referral by a PCP, 1-hour consultations for children and adolescents were conducted by faculty in English, with language interpreters as necessary. A consultation care model was used in which the consulting specialist would meet with the patient and would be joined by the PCP for 5 to 10 minutes at the end of the consultation to discuss options; after the initial meeting, the PCP would provide any ongoing mental health services. 16,18 All of the referring PCPs were sent detailed information by fax or e-mail immediately following the consultation, whether or not they had participated. Treatment recommendations included psychoeducation on medication and illness and diagnostic issues, exercise, and lifestyle issues, a referral for therapy or testing as needed, and up to three differing medication options from which to choose if necessary.

Data were collected on each consultation by trained research staff using a deidentified form. CBCL surveys were completed immediately before the video consultation, and follow-up CBCL surveys were completed at 3 months postintake by mailing surveys to the convenience sample of patients seen early in the project with a preaddressed, stamped return envelope. If follow-up surveys were not returned within 2 weeks, then UC Davis staff contacted parents by telephone up to three times. DSM-IV-TR diagnoses were recorded at each visit by the UC Davis Medical Center psychiatrists and psychologist using a semistructured interview. 15

## Data Analysis

Analysis of the diagnostic case mix, CBCL, and satisfaction surveys were completed at the end of the 12-month grant period using a repeated-measures multivariate analysis of variance design.

## RESULTS

Over the course of the eMH project, 289 adult and pediatric patients were seen via telecommunications technology. The ethnic composition of the project population was 87% white, 9% Hispanic, 2% African American, and 2% Native American. The present study sample includes the 139 children (younger than 18 years) who were seen and/or psychiatrically assessed by eMH

clinicians. The mean age of the study sample was 10.7 years (SD 4.1; range 1 Y 17). Patients younger than 18 had an average number of diagnoses of 1.3. Diagnoses fell into a variety of categories, with the largest group of children receiving diagnoses for attention-deficit/ hyperactivity disorder (36.2%), followed by mood disorders (28.1%). ## DISCUSSION

The results from this study provide both new information and parallel the current literature. The most important finding is that a wide range of psychiatric disorders in children were effectively diagnosed, as shown by our case-mix data, including anxiety, cognitive decline, depression, and psychosis in a multimedia (videoconferencing, telephone, and secure e-mail) eMH program. It is evident from the present results that the full range of child and adolescent psychiatric disorders can be assessed using videoconferencing. Satisfaction with this program was high, 16 as has been similarly reported in other studies. 18

An additional, although limited, finding was that a single videoconference psychiatric consultation was associated with improved mental health in our convenience sample 3 months later. It appears that when boys get better, they show an improvement in oppositional scores; when girls get better, they show improved mood scores. This is consistent with the adult literature, which reports that females tend to present with mood symptoms, whereas males tend to show depressive equivalent behaviors (e.g., drinking/risk taking). 19 However, given the nature of the convenience sample, the rate of attrition from the sample, and other study limitations, it is not possible at this time to state whether these statistical results are clinically or causally conclusive.

Successful intervention models and modules in telemedicine appear to have some common denominators. 15 The present study and the work of others internationally in Australia, Canada, and England suggest these features are mutual incentive for primary care and specialty partners (e.g., improved quality of care); commitment from physicians, staff, and administration to pursue telemedicine and alternate modes of consultation; systematic or multiple interventions that meet the needs of the distant site; a systems approach for the primary care system to monitor the flow of patients and measure outcomes; and consultants who are able to bridge the differing philosophies that may be unique to rural primary care and urban academic settings and to specific cultural groups. 20,21

This study has several limitations. It was not a randomized controlled trial with a single diagnosis, outcome measures are modest, generalizability to other settings may be difficult, and change in practice over time among participants may have been due to a variety of factors that were not assessed or controlled. In addition, our small sample size and analyses unadjusted for multiple comparisons may result in type I errors. In addition, substantial attrition was observed in the convenience sample. Although 58 CBCL surveys were completed at intake, only 41 were completed at 3-month follow-up. It is possible that factors influencing retention and dropout affected the results of our analysis, especially considering the small sample size. Finally, although there do appear to be statistically

significant improvements in the mental health status of participating children at 3-month follow up, a more tightly controlled study would have to be conducted to determine whether such improvements actually demonstrate clinically significant changes in the children \_ s behavior.

This report has important health service implications. It is evident that customized teleconsultation services are appropriate for children, and programs such as the eMH program at UC Davis could be particularly valuable to health care providers working in rural and remote areas. This is particularly true when considering that there is flexibility with consultative modalities, including telephone, e-mail, secure Web messaging, and live videoconferencing.

The main focus of this report was to describe the case mix of the children seen during the course of the eMH project and not to evaluate outcomes. Although it appears that there is some statistical improvement in the scores of the children in the study sample, it is beyond the scope of this nonrandomized uncontrolled study to make claims regarding the association between these two variables, and these results should not at present be taken to imply clinical improvement.

In summary, versatile eMH programs appear to B fit [ the needs of PCPs and no longer need to be limited to adults, but can be extended to child and adolescent populations, even in diverse rural communities. Much more research is needed on outcomes, as well as sex, cultural, and other sociodemographic factors. A stepped care system such as the one described here may be indicated to use valuable consultation resources judiciously or at least to accelerate the delivery of psychiatric services to the rural child and adolescent population. In addition, brief checklists, such as the CBCL, may be effective measurement tools for rural children and adolescents and could probably be routinely used by PCPs to determine whether psychiatric consultations are necessary for young patients with suspected mental health problems. However, the clinical implications of the use of such tools should certainly be evaluated further.
