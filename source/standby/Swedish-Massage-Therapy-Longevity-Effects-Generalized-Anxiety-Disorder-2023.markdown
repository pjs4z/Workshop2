## 1. Introduction

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) has a lifetime prevalence rate of approximately 5.8% and is the most common anxiety disorder reported in primary care settings (Kessler et al., 2005; Ormel et al., 1994; Wittchen et al., 2002). GAD is characterized by feeling worried, apprehensive, or psychically tense most of the time about routine every day events. Other symptoms commonly present in GAD include irritability, restlessness, insomnia, fatigue, muscle aches and tension, headaches, gastrointestinal complaints, and

Objective: Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is a prevalent and costly disorder, and many patients may prefer non-traditional treatment. A proof-of-concept study demonstrated the ef /uniFB01 cacy of Swedish Massage Therapy (SMT) as a monotherapy for treatment of GAD. Subjects were followed-up 6 e 12 months after study completion to evaluate post-treatment outcome.

Methods: Subjects were enrolled into a randomized, single-masked clinical trial between March of 2012 and May of 2013. Forty-seven untreated subjects with DSM-IV diagnosis of GAD were randomly assigned to 6 weeks of twice-a-week light touch (LT) followed by 6 weeks of twice-a-week SMT, or 12 weeks of twice-a-week SMT. The primary outcome measure was reduction in Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A) scores after six weeks of SMT versus LT. Qualifying participants received a follow-up survey to investigate whether the bene /uniFB01 ts of SMT for GAD were sustained.

Results: 28 of 40 subjects completed at least 12 sessions of SMT and were sent the follow-up survey. Of the 19 subjects with follow-up, nine (47%) reported no return of GAD symptoms up to 1 year after study completion. There were no differences between those randomized to 12 weeks SMT and those receiving 6 weeks LT followed by 6 weeks SMT. Of those reporting a return of some symptoms, 50% associated symptom return with a stressful life event.

Interpretation: In this /uniFB01 rst monotherapy trial of SMT for the treatment of GAD, follow-up results suggest that the bene /uniFB01 cial effects of SMT may last up to 1 year after end of treatment.

## 2. Methods

## 2.1. Trial design and participants

The trial design and participants for the initial 6 weeks of the study are fully described in Rapaport et al. (2016) and below. The study was a randomized, single-masked, two-arm clinical trial comparing six weeks of twice-weekly SMT vs. LT as monotherapy for GAD. The study was conducted in the Department of Psychiatry at Emory University in Atlanta, Georgia, between March 2012 and May 2013. Forty-seven subjects with GAD were recruited from the surrounding community by means of /uniFB02 yers, referrals, and word of mouth, and signed written informed consent was obtained. Randomization lists were created by a statistician at Emory who was not otherwise involved with the study, using randomly permuted block sizes of four or six subjects. Randomization was strati /uniFB01 ed by sex, to balance the distribution of males and females in the two treatment groups. Two groups of sealed envelopes, one for females and one for males, were prepared based on the strati /uniFB01 ed randomization lists and kept in a locked box with access only by the study coordinator. At the baseline visit, after con /uniFB01 rming the patient met all criteria for randomization, the study coordinator determined the treatment assignment by selecting the next sealed randomization envelope, based on male/female strati /uniFB01 cation. The coordinator opened the randomization envelope, showed the randomization to the therapist, and both coordinator and therapist signed off on the randomization in the randomization log, which was kept in a locked location by the study coordinator. The SMT and LT protocols are both described in detail in Larson et al. (2018). Brie /uniFB02 y, SMT and LT treatments occurred between 12:00 p.m. and 6:00 p.m. Interventions lasting 45 min were performed by licensed massage therapists from the Atlanta School of Massage, who adhered to a script that standardized their interactions with subjects and followed manualized treatment protocols for both interventions. Each session began with the subject draped with a sheet and in a prone position on a massage table while the therapist worked slowly down the body from the shoulders to the feet. The subject then turned over to the supine position and the therapist continued the protocol from the feet back up to the shoulders. Swedish massage techniques included ef /uniFB02 eurage (slow, rhythmic, continuous stroking), petrissage (slow, rhythmic kneading of underlying muscles), and tapotement (various forms of percussive touching/tapping). The same therapists performed the control light touch treatment, which consisted of the light laying on of hands, in the same sequence and for the same amount of time. Both SMT and LT were performed at a pressure level of 1 (Larson et al., 2018; Walton, 2011). Staff conducting outcome assessments was masked to subjects' treatment intervention, and participants were asked not to disclose their assigned treatment to those staff members. The study coordinator and therapists performing the interventions did not discuss subjects ' treatment assignment with other staff at any time during the study. The study statistician conducted primary outcome analyses using masked treatment codes. At the end of the 6-week treatment trial all subjects, regardless of initial treatment assignment, were offered the opportunity to receive an additional six weeks of twice-weekly SMT. A follow-up questionnaire (Appendix 1) was administered 6 e 12 months after completion of the 12-week study. Subjects had the option of completing the paper form and returning by mail, completing an electronic form, or completing the survey by telephone. The Institutional Review Board approved the study protocol. The study was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (identi /uniFB01 er: NCT01337713).

Subjects were medically healthy as demonstrated by a normal medical history and physical exam, met criteria for a primary diagnosis of current GAD as determined by a structured clinical interview for DSM-IV (SCID) (First et al., 1995), and had a Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A) (Hamilton,1959) total score &gt; 14. The HAM-A was chosen as the only assessment of anxiety because 1) it has validated subscales for both psychic anxiety and somatic anxiety, and 2) we wanted to minimize the assessment burden on subjects. Study personnel trained to administer the SCID and HAMA completed the assessments and the diagnosis of GAD was con /uniFB01 rmed by the study psychiatrist. Study personnel performing the HAM-A were masked to treatment condition. Subjects were permitted to have comorbid but secondary major depressive disorder, dysthymic disorder, or other anxiety disorders except obsessive compulsive disorder. Exclusion criteria included current suicidal ideation, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, borderline personality disorder, illicit drug use, current psychotropic medication use, current participation in psychotherapy, pregnancy, shift work, current dieting, active medical problems, excessive regular use of alcohol (more than two 5-oz glasses of wine or equivalents/day), or a history of binge drinking (more than seven drinks/24 h period) within the last six months. Participants had to have completed at least 12 sessions of SMT to be eligible to participate in the follow-up study.

## 2.2. Post-study questionnaire

The post-study questionnaire (Appendix 1) was created after completion of the main study, as a way to obtain exploratory information about the return or worsening of symptoms, the timing of when these symptoms worsened or returned, post-study treatment (response to treatment and comparison of that treatment to SMT), comments about how SMT impacted their anxiety, 4 questions about symptoms of anxiety, and 4 questions investigating quality of life (QOL) and function. The 4 questions investigating anxiety symptoms asked ' howoften in the last 7 days have you: felt tense, been worried, been fatigued, had trouble sleeping, ' and employed 5 scalors: 1 (never), 2 (rarely), 3 (sometimes), 4 (often) and 5 (always). The 4 questions investigating QOL and functioning asked how subjects rated their physical well-being, overall quality of life, ability to deal with stressful situations, and overall productivity (work, home, and social life) over the last 7 days and employed 5 scalors: 1 (very good), 2 (good), 3 (so-so), 4 (not very

good), and 5 (poor). Statistical analyses were carried out using GraphPad software. A 2-tailed a level of 0.05 was used to determine statistical signi /uniFB01 cance.

## 3. Results

109 subjects were screened for the acute treatment study, 47 were randomized and 40 subjects had at least one intervention session and were included in the analysis of the initial 6 weeks of the study. Participants had to have completed at least 12 sessions of SMT to be eligible for follow-up data collection. Twenty-eight participants who met this criterion were sent the follow-up survey 6 e 12 months after completing the study, and 19 subjects completed the survey (Fig. 1). Of these 19 subjects, 13 had completed 6 weeks of SMT (n ¼ 3 with SMT only and n ¼ 10 after 6 weeks of LT) and 6 subjects had completed 12 weeks of SMT. Subject demographics are shown in Table 1.

When asked whether their symptoms returned or got worse,

47% (9/19) reported no return of any symptoms, while 53% (10/19) reported a return/worsening of symptoms. No participants reported that they had continued to have GAD nor had an exacerbation of GAD. The timing of the development of symptoms after the end of treatment varied: 4 reported developing symptoms in under 6 months; 3 reported developing symptoms at 6 months or greater; 1 reported developing symptoms at 1 year; 2 could not remember. Five out of the 10 participants who experienced return or worsening of their GAD symptoms reported having life events that were responsible for recurrence of symptoms.

Fifty percent (5/10) participants who experienced a return of symptoms chose to seek treatment for their anxiety. Two subjects selected to have psychotherapy, one chose yoga, and one opted for medication. One participant elected to use massage for symptom control. Three out of /uniFB01 ve subjects indicated that SMT was more bene /uniFB01 cial than their subsequent treatment. ## 4. Discussion

GAD is a common, chronic and debilitating condition (Keller, 2002). GAD is associated with increased sympathetic nervous system activity as well as decreased parasympathetic nervous system activity (Newman et al., 2013; Pittig et al., 2013). Further, basal activity of neural circuits that control peripheral targets of stress response (Davis et al.,1993) is elevated in those with GAD. Although both pharmacological and psychotherapeutic treatments for GAD are available, the response and remission rates are inadequate and leave many individuals with ongoing symptoms and impairment (Cuijpers et al., 2014; Pollack, 2009; Rickels and Rynn, 2002; Szkodny et al., 2014; Tyrer and Baldwin, 2006).

The current study provides follow-up information on subjects who underwent monotherapy treatment with SMT for GAD. At the end of 6 weeks, subjects with GAD who received twice-weekly SMT demonstrated greater statistically and clinically signi /uniFB01 cant improvement on clinician-rated and self-report ratings of both anxiety relative to the active control condition (Rapaport et al., 2016). We then assessed the impact of twelve additional sessions of SMT on the overall change in anxiety, with preliminary /uniFB01 ndings suggesting that subjects who continued with SMT for a second twelve sessions did continue to experience less anxiety as measured by the HAM-A (Rapaport et al., 2021).

This preliminary descriptive analysis of subjects who participated in the pilot study of SMT monotherapy for GAD suggests that there may be some enduring bene /uniFB01 t to massage therapy both in terms of symptom reduction as well as improvement in QOL and functioning for at least some individuals. Forty seven percent of respondents reported that they had not had a return of symptoms at 6 e 12 months follow-up. The 53% of subjects who did endorse the return of some symptoms generally suggested that the symptoms were present ' some of the time ' . Importantly, it is clear from Table 3 that all the subjects in this follow-up assessment indicated that their well-being, QOL, and productivity were in the very good to good range. This suggests that even those subjects who had a return of some symptoms were not so bothered by their symptoms to meet the functional disability requirement necessary to meet criteria for GAD. Particularly considering the chronic and persistent nature of GAD, these very preliminary /uniFB01 ndings may suggest that even a brief course of SMT may have longer term bene /uniFB01 t.

There are important limitations to small, unfunded follow-up studies. There is always concern about a selection bias. First, as acknowledged in previous work (Rapaport et al., 2016), only a subset of individuals suffering from GAD are interested in participating in research and a more limited subset would be interested in participating in a study of a manual therapy for GAD. Second, respondents had to agree to complete the follow-up survey without being compensated. Further, six subjects who completed at least 6weeks of twice-weekly massage were lost to follow-up and did not complete surveys and so we do not know how their responses might in /uniFB02 uence our preliminary observations. A second concern is that the length of follow-up was a minimum of 6 months but only extended up to one year, thus it is possible that more individuals might have had a return of symptoms at a later time. Another criticism of this preliminary study might be our decision to forgo employing standardized ratings scales, but we felt that these brief Likert scales covering 4 key symptoms (two psychological and two physical) of GAD and 4 brief measures of QOL and functioning would give us a reasonable brief description of our subjects ' posttreatment status. We deliberately did not use inferential statistics since we did not have well described hypotheses for this follow-up but rather consider these descriptive data hypothesis-generating.

In summary, this follow-up study of patients with GAD who had received between 12 and up to 24 sessions of SMT suggests that there may be a sustained bene /uniFB01 t of massage therapy for some people with GAD. These preliminary data suggest that SMT improved anxiety symptoms, QOL, and functioning for patients with GAD. A larger, systematic, prospective study of the sustained post-treatment bene /uniFB01 ts of manualized massage for GAD is warranted.
